Marketing School Changed How I See Every Brand I Love

You can never just be a consumer again. Consider yourself warned.

Chloe Rudd·Jan 30, 2026·7 min read

There is a specific kind of ruination that comes with studying marketing. Before, you could walk into a Target, grab your things, and leave. Now? Now you're standing in the cereal aisle for fifteen minutes analyzing the kerning on a Cheerios box and muttering about brand equity to yourself. My roommate has asked me to stop doing this in public.

I'm a junior in the marketing program at BYU, and somewhere between Brand Management and Consumer Behavior, my brain got rewired. I can't look at an ad without mentally pulling it apart. I can't scroll Instagram without auditing someone's content strategy. It is a blessing and a curse, and I am writing this post because I need people to understand what they're getting into when they sign up for this major.

"Once you learn what a brand is really doing, you see it everywhere — in the color of a coffee cup, the font on a fast food menu, the specific feeling a store plays music to manufacture."

The Grocery Store Is Now a Field Study

I went home to Arizona for fall break and my mom asked me to pick up a few things from the store. I came back forty-five minutes later with half the list because I got distracted analyzing the private label branding strategy of the Safeway store brand. In my defense, it was genuinely interesting. In my mom's defense, she just needed eggs.

The thing about consumer packaged goods is that every single decision — the color, the typeface, the placement on the shelf, the language on the back panel — is intentional. Nothing is an accident. Once you know that, you start asking "why" about everything, and grocery shopping becomes a very slow, very nerdy experience.

Social Media Hit Different After Consumer Behavior

I took a Consumer Behavior course last semester and I genuinely think it should come with a warning label. We spent weeks on psychological triggers, decision-making frameworks, and the science of persuasion. Now when I see an Instagram ad, I can name exactly which cognitive bias it's trying to exploit. It's like knowing a magic trick — once you see how it's done, you can't unsee it.

The one that got me was scarcity. "Only 3 left in stock!" "This offer expires tonight!" I used to fall for this constantly. Now I just sit there nodding slowly like some kind of unimpressed marketing professor who has seen it all before. My friends find this insufferable at the checkout. I find it valuable. We've agreed to disagree.

Why I'm Actually Glad My Brain Broke

Here's the thing — yes, it makes me annoying at the grocery store. Yes, I now have opinions about logo redesigns that no one asked for. But it also means I'm genuinely good at this. When I look at a brand campaign and I can tell you what's working and what isn't, that's not just trivia. That's a skill set. That's what I want to do for a living.

Building brands is one of the most creative and strategic things a business can do. It's storytelling and psychology and design and culture all wrapped together. And the fact that I can't turn my brain off around it? I think that just means I picked the right major. Even if it makes me a nightmare to take to the grocery store.
