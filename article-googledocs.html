Google Docs vs. Microsoft Word: A Deeply Personal Debate

I betrayed my beloved Microsoft Suite and I have thoughts about it.

Chloe Rudd·Nov 15, 2025·4 min read

I want to be upfront about something: I am a Microsoft Suite person. Have been my whole life. Word, Excel, PowerPoint — I was raised on these programs and I have remained loyal to them in the way you remain loyal to a hometown sports team even when they are absolutely not doing well. It's not rational. It's identity.

So when I started drafting my first blog posts on Google Docs, I want you to understand the psychological weight of that decision. I didn't take it lightly. I agonized. But Google Docs has this document tabs feature that is genuinely brilliant for organizing a multi-post project and I am not too proud to admit when something works.

"My main problem with Google Docs is that my cursor always turns white and I can never find it. I will be accepting solutions in the comments. I will be eternally grateful."

The Case for Microsoft Word (My Homeland)

Word is just better at making things look professional. The formatting options are more robust, the templates are more polished, and it doesn't randomly change my fonts when I paste something in from somewhere else. It also doesn't require internet, which sounds like a minor thing until you're on a flight trying to finish an assignment and Google Docs just sits there looking at you.

There's also something about the interface that feels more serious. When I open Word I feel like I'm about to do Real Work. When I open Google Docs I feel like I'm about to collaborate on a group project, which is a vibe I cannot always access on demand.

The Reluctant Case for Google Docs

The tabs. I cannot overstate how useful the document tabs feature is for a project like this blog. I can have one Google Doc with a tab for each post, a tab for my content calendar, a tab for ideas I'm not ready to write yet. It's organized in a way that Word simply doesn't replicate without opening seventeen separate files and losing them all in your downloads folder.

Also, collaboration is seamless. If I ever want someone to read something before I publish it — a friend, my twin who will tell me if something is unhinged — I can just send a link. No attachments. No version control nightmares. It's genuinely convenient and I will not pretend otherwise.

My Conclusion, Which Satisfies No One

I draft in Google Docs and I do not feel great about it. I do final formatting in Word when I need things to look professional. I am a woman of two document processing homes and I've made peace with that. But if someone at Google could fix the white cursor situation, I would consider this whole thing resolved. That's all I'm asking. One cursor. Visible. Please.
