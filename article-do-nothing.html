On Learning to Do Nothing Without Guilt

Rest isn't laziness. It took me way too long to figure that out.

Chloe Rudd·Feb 14, 2026·6 min read

We have been conditioned, from a very young age, to equate busyness with worth. If you're not doing something, you're falling behind. If you're sitting still, you must be lazy. If your calendar has a blank Tuesday, something has gone wrong. I spent most of my freshman and sophomore year at BYU living by this logic — and I was exhausted.

The thing about being a marketing student is that everyone around you is hustling. Building their personal brand, landing internships, joining every club, networking at 8am on a Saturday. And I was right there with them, convinced that if I slowed down for even a second, I would somehow become irrelevant.

"Somewhere between my third club meeting of the week and my second cold brew of the day, I realized I couldn't remember the last time I had just... sat outside."

The Moment I Actually Stopped

It was a Sunday in October. The weather in Provo was doing that thing it does in fall where it's genuinely beautiful for about two weeks before turning absolutely against you. I had a list of things I was supposed to do. I did none of them. I sat on my porch for two hours and watched the mountains and drank my coffee while it was still hot.

And nothing bad happened. The world did not end. My GPA did not spontaneously combust. My LinkedIn connections did not evaporate. I just... sat there. And it was the most rested I had felt in months.

Why We're So Bad at This

I think part of it is social media. When everyone's highlight reel is a constant stream of productivity and achievement, doing nothing feels like a personality flaw. But rest is not the absence of ambition. It is what makes ambition sustainable. You cannot pour from an empty cup — and yes I know that's the most Pinterest thing I've ever said, but it's also just true.

The other part is identity. So much of who I thought I was had become tied up in what I was doing. When I stopped doing things, I got a little bit scared about who I was without the doing. That's a bigger conversation for a different post, but I'll just say: the answer is that you're still you. You just get a little quieter, and a little more honest.

What I'm Still Learning

I haven't figured this out completely. I still feel a little twinge of guilt when I watch TV on a weeknight before all my work is done. I still sometimes open my laptop on Sundays out of pure anxiety. But I'm getting better at noticing when I'm pushing through exhaustion out of habit rather than necessity — and choosing differently.

If you're reading this with a to-do list a mile long and the quiet voice in the back of your head saying you should be doing something more productive: maybe this is the something more productive. Rest is not a reward. It's a requirement. You're allowed to just exist for a minute.
